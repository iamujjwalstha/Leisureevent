What is Risk
Management?

The optimization of acceptable risks and the minimization or elimination of unacceptable risks across all aspects of an event.

01

Identify

Spot all possible risks — legal, business, environmental, operational — before they happen.

02

Develop strategies to avoid, transfer, minimize, or optimize each identified risk.

04

Continuously revisit your risk assessment and update it as circumstances change.

Legal
Legal Risks
⚖️

What is Tort Law?

A civil wrong that causes harm or loss to another person. In events, it covers injuries to participants, spectators, or third parties.

🎯

Intentional Liability

  • Knowingly allowing unsafe conditions
  • Deliberately misleading participants about safety
  • Willful disregard of known hazards
⚠️

Unintentional Liability

  • Spectator hit by cheese wheel
  • Participant collides with bystander
  • Inadequate barriers causing crowd injury
🏛️

Professional Duties

  • Provide adequate safety briefings
  • Ensure medical staff on site
  • Maintain safe spectator zones
  • Conduct proper risk assessments
📋

5 Elements of a Valid Contract

  • Offer — one party proposes terms
  • Acceptance — other party agrees
  • Consideration — something of value exchanged
  • Intention — legal commitment intended
  • Capacity — both parties can legally sign
🚚

Supplier Contracts Must Include

  • What is being supplied
  • Delivery timeline
  • Quality standards
  • Payment terms
  • Penalty clauses for failure
  • Termination conditions
🤝

Sponsorship Contracts

  • Sponsorship fee & payment schedule
  • Exclusivity clause
  • Branding rights
  • Deliverables from organizer
  • Force majeure clause
  • Dispute resolution method
⚡

Breach of Contract

If a supplier fails to deliver, the organizer can claim:

  • Damages — financial compensation
  • Specific performance — court order to fulfill
  • Termination — end the agreement legally
©️

Copyright

Protects creative works automatically.

  • Official photographs & videos
  • Event website content
  • Promotional materials
  • Music played at event (requires license)

Duration Life + 70 years

™️

Trademark

Protects brand names and logos.

  • Event name registration
  • Logo protection
  • Slogans (e.g. "Chase the Cheese")
  • Prevents counterfeit merchandise

Duration Indefinite if renewed

📺

Media Rights

  • TV broadcast rights
  • Online streaming rights
  • Social media content rights
  • International distribution rights

Currently unprotected = major missed revenue

🛍️

Merchandise Rights

  • License name/logo for products
  • Stop unauthorized sellers
  • Cease & desist for infringers
  • Register in multiple categories

Risk Counterfeit goods sold freely

💼

Bailment

Temporary transfer of possession of property. If organizers take custody of attendee items (coat check, storage), they have a duty of care over those items.

🏷️

Title & Ownership

Who legally owns property on event grounds? Disputes can arise over equipment, merchandise, or items left behind after the event.

🔒

Organizer Liability

  • Post "not responsible for lost items" signage
  • If negligent security caused theft — liability arises
  • Vendors should carry own property insurance
  • Advise participants to leave valuables at home
🏔️

Land/Venue Damage

  • Post-event restoration responsibility
  • Ground damage costs from crowds
  • Landowner agreement must specify liability
  • Insurance must cover property damage

Criminal negligence occurs when organizers show reckless disregard for safety beyond simple mistakes.

  • Ignoring council warnings about unsafe ground conditions
  • Allowing an intoxicated person to participate knowing they could harm others
  • Failing to provide adequate medical coverage despite knowing injury is likely

Consequence Could lead to manslaughter charges if a death occurs

  • Pickpocketing in large crowds
  • Theft from vendor stalls
  • Unauthorized merchandise sellers stealing the event brand/name
  • Ticket scalping or fraudulent ticket sales

Mitigation: Security personnel, CCTV, clear anti-theft policies in vendor contracts

  • Fake ticket sellers charging entry to a free event
  • Sponsors being misled about expected attendance numbers
  • Falsifying safety compliance documents to obtain permits
  • False advertising of event features or performers

Consequence Criminal fraud charges against the organizer

  • A participant deliberately pushing a competitor
  • Crowd fights among intoxicated spectators
  • Security staff using excessive force
  • Deliberate redirection of a cheese wheel into the crowd

Intentional = Criminal assault. Accidental = Falls under negligence/liability territory.

Business
Business Risks
  • No formal bookkeeping or financial records
  • Cash transactions from vendors are untracked
  • No audit trail — making fraud easy to hide
  • Tax obligations on vendor income may go unreported
  • No stable income stream — entirely dependent on goodwill
  • Unexpected costs from medical emergencies or legal claims
  • Sponsorship withdrawal mid-planning
  • Low attendance due to bad weather killing vendor revenue
  • No financial reserves or contingency fund
  • A serious injury or fatality caught on camera going viral negatively
  • Negative media framing — irresponsible, dangerous, reckless
  • Counterfeit merchandise damaging authentic brand image
  • A sponsor with a controversial reputation associating with the event
  • Social media backlash from safety advocacy groups
  • No official marketing = inconsistent messaging
  • Third parties spreading misinformation about dates or location
  • Poor attendance due to lack of promotion
  • Unofficial ticket sellers creating confusion
  • Missed sponsorship revenue due to no formal sales pitch process
  • Cheese delivery failure — wrong type, late arrival, insufficient quantity
  • Barrier/fencing not installed on time creating safety gaps
  • Medical team delayed or understaffed on event day
  • Crowd flow bottlenecks at the hill entrance
  • Parking and transport chaos with international crowds
  • No clear communication system between staff
  • Hill access road too narrow for emergency vehicles
  • Heavy rain — hill becomes dangerously slippery, injuries multiply
  • Extreme heat — dehydration risk for participants and spectators
  • Ground erosion from thousands of feet damaging the hillside permanently
  • Waste management — litter left by large crowds harming local ecosystem
  • Noise pollution disturbing nearby residents and wildlife
  • Potential legal environmental liability if land is damaged
  • Volunteer burnout — event relies heavily on informal volunteers with no HR structure
  • No clear leadership — decision-making is chaotic
  • Cultural insensitivity — international crowds may disrespect local traditions
  • Sponsor values mismatch with the event's heritage feel
  • Communication breakdown between landowner, local council, and event community
  • Risk of the event losing its authentic local identity as it grows commercially

Step 03
Risk Response
Strategies
Strategy 01

Avoid

Eliminate the risk entirely by changing plans or cancelling risky elements.

Cooper's Hill: Cancel the race entirely when ground conditions are dangerously wet.
Strategy 02

Transfer

Pass the risk to another party — typically through insurance, waivers, or subcontracting.

Cooper's Hill: Require participant liability waivers; purchase event insurance.
Strategy 03

Minimize

Reduce the likelihood or impact of the risk through preventative measures.

Cooper's Hill: Add on-site medical staff, install barriers, run safety briefings.
Strategy 04

Optimize

Turn a risk into an opportunity — accept it and find a way to benefit from it.

Cooper's Hill: Use viral injury footage (with consent) as part of marketing strategy.

💡

📅
